Industry Reform Proposal — Victoria

A practical licensing pathway for residential renovation carpentry

Victoria's licensing system does not currently reflect the work performed by highly experienced renovation carpenters. This proposal seeks to create a dedicated Registered Residential Carpenter (RRC) pathway — a renovation-focused licence that recognises real on-site experience while fully protecting builder scope.

The Problem

Victoria's licensing framework does not include a dedicated renovation carpentry tier.

Residential renovation represents a significant and distinct segment of Victoria's construction activity. Bathroom and kitchen renovations, internal refurbishments, deck and pergola construction, and window and door upgrades are performed at scale by small-business carpenters across the state. This work differs in nature, scope, and risk profile from new home construction and large-scale structural building.

The current licensing framework does not include a pathway for experienced renovation carpenters to directly contract and manage non-structural work in existing homes. A carpenter with substantial on-site renovation experience who routinely delivers bathroom renovations, kitchen upgrades, or internal refurbishments must operate as a subcontractor under a registered builder, or manage projects without a licence appropriate to the scope of the work.

A dedicated licensing tier would improve regulatory fit, strengthen consumer protection, and provide a clear and bounded pathway for experienced practitioners working within a renovation-specific scope.

The registered building licence remains the correct instrument for new construction, major structural works, extensions, and large-scale developments. This proposal seeks to add a complementary renovation-focused tier — it does not alter, reduce, or qualify existing registered builder scope in any way.

The Solution

A Registered Residential Carpenter (RRC) licence.

This proposal seeks to establish a Registered Residential Carpenter (RRC) licence as a new middle tier in Victoria's residential licensing framework. The RRC would sit between a qualified carpenter (who works as a subcontractor) and a registered builder (who manages all classes of residential construction).

The RRC would allow appropriately experienced carpenters to directly contract, price, and manage renovation work in existing homes — within a clearly defined, non-structural and light-structural scope.

Renovation-focused scope

Bathrooms, kitchens, decks, pergolas, verandas, internal renovations, windows and doors, and general non-structural work in existing residential buildings.

Experience-based entry

Entry based on demonstrated on-site experience, trade qualification, and a competency assessment — recognising the real skills renovation carpenters already hold.

Builder scope fully protected

New homes, major structural works, extensions, multi-storey buildings, and large developments remain exclusively within registered builder scope. No change.

A possible future optional endorsement — the RRC-S — could allow limited structural renovation work under strict conditions and competency requirements. This is a secondary consideration and would require further regulatory consultation.

Builder Scope Protection

Builder responsibilities are explicitly preserved.

The RRC proposal is designed to complement, not compete with, registered builders. The following work would remain exclusively within registered builder scope under this proposal:

  • Construction of new homes
  • Major structural works (load-bearing alterations, footings, frames at scale)
  • Major extensions and additions to existing homes
  • Multi-storey residential and commercial buildings
  • Subdivisions, developments, and large-scale projects

This reform does not alter registered builder scope. It creates a clearly bounded additional tier for renovation-specific work in existing homes. New construction, structural works, extensions, and large-scale projects remain exclusively within registered builder scope.

Industry associations representing builders are encouraged to engage with this proposal. The intent is to strengthen the overall system by improving regulatory fit across all tiers of residential carpentry and construction.

Housing & Renovation Capacity

Why this matters for housing delivery in Victoria.

Renovation of existing housing stock is a critical lever for improving housing amenity, liveability, and supply outcomes across Victoria. Bathroom and kitchen renovations, internal upgrades, and ancillary structure construction (decks, pergolas, verandas) are conducted at significant volume by small-business carpenters across the state.

A fit-for-purpose licensing pathway for this cohort would:

  • Increase the number of appropriately licensed practitioners available for residential renovation work
  • Improve consumer protection by bringing experienced carpenters into a regulated licensing framework
  • Reduce friction in the renovation market by clarifying who can contract work directly
  • Support small business growth and trade recognition across the renovation sector
  • Contribute to housing improvement at scale without requiring new construction

This reform aligns with broader Victorian Government objectives around housing delivery, skilled trades recognition, and consumer protection in the construction sector.
