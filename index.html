Carpenters for Housing Reform Victoria

Industry-led reform proposal for a Registered Residential Carpenter pathway.

Victoria’s residential licensing system no longer matches the economic and practical reality of modern renovations.

Carpenters for Housing Reform Victoria is advocating for a Registered Residential Carpenter (RRC) pathway — a practical, regulated middle-tier licence for highly experienced carpenters managing defined residential renovation work, with a future pathway toward limited structural renovation endorsement in existing homes.

The Problem

Victoria’s current framework generally pushes domestic building work over $10,000 into the registered builder pathway, while structural work requires a registered practitioner regardless of cost.

$10k Threshold where a major domestic building contract framework kicks in
$20k–$40k Typical bathroom renovation in today’s market
$25k–$70k Typical kitchen renovation in today’s market

The result is a clear mismatch: many experienced carpenters already coordinate renovation delivery, solve existing-house problems, sequence trades, and manage practical structural issues in older homes — yet the system does not clearly recognise that specialist renovation role.

Why reform matters now

Modern renovation work has outgrown an outdated licensing structure.

High-value renovations are now ordinary across Victoria. Yet the current framework still treats much of this work as if it belongs only inside the builder pathway, even when it is renovation-specific, highly practical, and already managed in reality by experienced carpenters.

The Solution

The answer is not to weaken standards. The answer is to create a clearer, safer, more practical licensing pathway that reflects how renovation work already operates in Victoria.

Level 1

Qualified Carpenter

A qualified carpenter with completed apprenticeship training, broad trade capability, and the foundational skills of the carpentry trade.

Level 2

Registered Residential Carpenter (RRC)

A renovation-focused middle-tier licence for highly experienced carpenters, recognising practical project delivery, compliance knowledge, trade coordination, and real on-site responsibility.

  • Minimum 10+ years experience
  • Demonstrated renovation project experience
  • Understanding of compliance, permits, and sequencing
  • Coordination of licensed trades where required
Future Endorsement

Structural Renovation Endorsement

A future advanced endorsement pathway for highly experienced renovation carpenters undertaking defined small-scale structural work in existing homes within clear regulatory, permit, and compliance limits.

  • Limited load-bearing and structural alterations
  • Existing residential homes only
  • Permit-linked and scope-limited
  • Not for new homes or major structural builds

The core idea

Recognise the experienced renovation carpenter as a real professional category in Victoria, with a legal pathway that reflects real capability, real responsibility, and the specialist nature of renovation work in existing homes.

RRC Policy Position

The Registered Residential Carpenter proposal is based on one core principle: Victoria should recognise highly experienced renovation carpenters through a clearly defined licence pathway, while keeping builder scope protected.

Renovation-focused recognition

The RRC pathway is intended to recognise experienced carpenters already managing defined residential renovation work in practice.

Future structural endorsement

A future endorsed pathway could allow limited structural renovation work in existing homes within strict scope, permit, and compliance limits.

Builder scope remains protected

Builders remain responsible for new homes, major structural construction, major extensions, multi-storey buildings, and large developments.

Clear Division of Responsibility

This reform is not about replacing builders. It is about creating the right licence for the right work, with clear boundaries and protected builder scope.

Builders remain responsible for

  • All new homes
  • Structural construction and major framing
  • Major extensions
  • Multi-storey buildings
  • Large residential developments

RRC holders could manage

  • Bathroom renovations
  • Kitchen renovations
  • Window and door upgrades
  • Decks, pergolas, and verandas
  • Internal renovations and rectification work
  • Non-structural renovation-focused residential work

Future structural endorsement could cover

  • Defined small load-bearing wall changes
  • Limited structural stud wall work
  • Existing-home structural alterations within set limits
  • Permit-linked, standards-based scope only
  • Not new-home framing or open-ended structural authority

Why This Makes Sense

Renovation work in existing houses is a specialist skill. It is different from clean-sheet new-home construction. Many of the hardest parts of renovation are not just build tasks — they are problem-solving tasks: tying into old work, correcting defects, sequencing trades, handling surprises behind walls, and making practical decisions inside real homes.

Victoria already has experienced carpenters doing this work every day. The proposal is to recognise that specialist capability properly instead of forcing everything into a builder-only binary.

Benefits

For Government

Improves regulatory fit, recognises real workforce capability, and helps unlock renovation capacity without lowering standards.

For Industry

Recognises experienced tradespeople, reduces bottlenecks, and lets builders stay focused on new homes and larger structural projects.

For Homeowners

Improves access to qualified renovation specialists, with clearer accountability and a system better matched to real project types.

Why builders should support this

This reform is designed to protect builder scope, not shrink it. Builders remain responsible for new homes, major structural construction, major extensions, and larger projects, while a renovation-focused pathway helps separate smaller renovation work and improve overall industry capacity.

Built from real renovation experience

Renovation carpentry is its own specialist skill set.

Existing homes require judgment, sequencing, rectification, and practical structural understanding that often sits most strongly with highly experienced renovation carpenters. This reform is about recognising that reality within a safer legal framework.

Long‑Term Vision

A clearer, more practical licensing structure for renovation work

Victoria’s licensing system can better recognise the real‑world capability of experienced renovation carpenters. The long‑term vision is a structure that protects builder scope while creating a practical, renovation‑focused pathway for tradespeople who specialise in existing homes.

A defined renovation pathway

The Registered Residential Carpenter (RRC) provides a clear, non‑structural renovation licence for highly experienced carpenters. It recognises capability in bathrooms, kitchens, decks, pergolas, verandas, internal renovations, and other existing‑home work that does not require a builder.

A future structural endorsement

A future endorsement could allow limited structural renovation work in existing homes under strict limits, clear safeguards, and permit‑linked boundaries — without blurring the line between renovation carpenters and builders. This supports renovation capacity while maintaining strong consumer protection.

This vision strengthens the renovation workforce, improves regulatory fit, and supports housing opportunity across Victoria — while keeping major structural and new‑home construction firmly within builder scope.
