The Proposal

A smarter renovation licensing model for Victoria.

The current domestic building threshold no longer reflects the cost of ordinary renovation work. The proposed Registered Residential Carpenter pathway would recognise experienced carpenters already coordinating renovation projects, while preserving the essential role of registered builders in new homes, structural work, and major construction.

$10k Current threshold where builder contracting rules often begin to apply
$20k–$40k Typical bathroom renovation range in today’s market
$25k–$70k Typical kitchen renovation range in today’s market

Problem

Many common renovations now exceed the current threshold, even when the project is primarily carpentry-led and already managed by experienced tradespeople on site.

Proposed solution

Introduce a regulated renovation-focused pathway for highly experienced carpenters with clear scope limits, experience requirements, and consumer protections.

Outcome

Reduced bottlenecks, stronger recognition of real renovation experience, and better delivery of smaller residential projects across Victoria.

This proposal is not about replacing builders. It is about creating a clear, regulated middle path that reflects how renovation work already operates in the real world.

Why Reform Matters Now

The market has changed. The licensing system has not.

Victoria’s current domestic building framework no longer reflects the real cost, structure, and delivery model of ordinary residential renovation work.

Modern renovation costs

Bathrooms, kitchens, decks, and internal renovations now regularly exceed thresholds that no longer reflect what most people would consider normal renovation work.

Real work is already happening

Experienced carpenters already coordinate renovation delivery on site every day — sequencing trades, managing workflow, and delivering practical outcomes for homeowners.

A practical reform opportunity

A regulated middle-tier licence would improve the fit between licensing, real-world experience, and the modern residential renovation market in Victoria.

Proposed Division of Responsibility

A stronger system defines the right licence for the right work.

This pathway is renovation-focused. It is not intended to cover new-home framing or new-home construction. Builders remain essential. Licensed plumbers and electricians remain unchanged.

Builders remain responsible for

  • All new homes
  • Structural construction
  • Major extensions
  • Multi-storey buildings
  • Large residential developments

Registered Residential Carpenters could manage

  • Bathroom, laundry, and kitchen renovations
  • Window and door upgrades or replacement
  • Internal fit-off carpentry and finish work
  • Decks, pergolas, and verandas where applicable
  • Repair and rectification works within defined limits
  • Small granny flats or studios up to 30 m², single storey

Field Evidence

What this looks like in the real world.

This reform is based on how renovation work already operates on site across Victoria. These examples help show the gap between regulation and reality.

Example 1

Bathroom renovation

A bathroom renovation may involve demolition, framing adjustments, sheeting, fix-outs, coordination of plumbing and electrical, waterproofing sequencing, and finishing trades — often led on site by an experienced carpenter.

Example 2

Kitchen and internal renovation

Many kitchen and internal renovation projects require carpentry-led coordination across multiple trades while remaining renovation-focused rather than new-home or structural building work.

Example 3

Decks, pergolas, and external works

Outdoor structures and associated upgrades regularly exceed legacy value thresholds, even where the work remains practical, trade-led, and well within the capability of highly experienced carpenters.

Safeguards & Common Questions

A practical reform must protect standards, builders, and consumers.

The Registered Residential Carpenter proposal is designed to improve regulatory fit while preserving builder responsibilities, consumer protection, and clear scope boundaries.

Will this reduce building standards?

No. It strengthens accountability by bringing renovation-specialist responsibility into a clearer regulated framework rather than leaving practical site reality outside formal recognition.

Does this replace registered builders?

No. Builders remain responsible for new homes, structural work, major extensions, and larger residential projects. This proposal is designed to support, not replace, builder licensing.

Does this add unnecessary complexity?

No. It simplifies a mismatch that already exists by recognising a missing middle tier between qualified carpenter and licensed builder for appropriate renovation work.

How would the scope stay clear?

By excluding structural construction, major extensions, and builder-led work while keeping plumbing and electrical with licensed trades.

What about homeowner protection?

Any serious reform should include insurance, written contracts, compliance obligations, approvals where required, and clear accountability for the work being performed.

Why is this needed now?

Because the economic reality of modern renovations has changed. Bathrooms, kitchens, decks, and internal works now regularly exceed thresholds that no longer reflect normal renovation practice.
