Victoria’s residential licensing framework does not adequately recognise experienced renovation carpenters or reflect how renovation work actually operates.

Carpenters for Housing Reform Victoria is advocating for a Registered Residential Carpenter (RRC) pathway — a practical, regulated licence for highly experienced carpenters managing defined residential renovation work in existing homes.

The Problem

Victoria’s current licensing framework generally requires domestic building work above $10,000 to be contracted through the registered builder pathway. Structural work requires a registered practitioner regardless of cost.

The practical result is a mismatch between regulation and reality. Standard bathroom renovations now commonly cost between $20,000 and $40,000. Kitchen renovations regularly reach $25,000 to $70,000. These are ordinary renovation projects in existing homes — not major structural builds — yet the current framework does not provide a clear, appropriate licence for the experienced carpenters who already manage and deliver this work.

Many highly experienced renovation carpenters already coordinate project delivery, sequence trades, manage compliance obligations, and solve complex existing-house problems on site every day. The licensing system does not recognise that specialist role.

The Proposed Solution

The answer is not to lower standards. It is to create a clearer, more appropriate licensing pathway that reflects how renovation work already operates in Victoria.

The proposed Registered Residential Carpenter (RRC) is a middle-tier licence for highly experienced carpenters. It would:

  • Require a minimum of ten years of demonstrated renovation experience
  • Recognise competency in project delivery, trade coordination, compliance, and permits
  • Apply exclusively to renovation work in existing residential homes
  • Operate within clear scope limits that preserve builder responsibilities

A future optional endorsement (RRC-S) could provide for limited structural renovation work in existing homes, subject to strict competency requirements, permit linkage, and defined scope limits.

The proposal aligns licensing requirements with the real-world work performed by renovation carpenters, improves regulatory fit, and strengthens the accountability framework around renovation delivery.

Builder Scope Protection

This proposal is not anti-builder. Builder scope is explicitly protected and remains unchanged under this proposal.

Registered builders remain solely responsible for:

Builder scope (unchanged)

  • All new home construction
  • Major structural construction and framing
  • Major extensions
  • Multi-storey residential buildings
  • Large residential developments

RRC scope (proposed)

  • Bathroom renovations
  • Kitchen renovations
  • Decks, pergolas, and verandas
  • Window and door upgrades
  • Internal renovations and rectification
  • Non-structural renovation work in existing homes

The RRC pathway is intentionally limited to renovation work in existing homes. It does not extend to new homes, major structural construction, or work that currently requires a registered builder.

Why This Matters for Housing and Renovation Capacity

Renovation of existing homes is a critical part of Victoria’s housing ecosystem. It improves housing stock, increases density, and provides homeowners with practical options for adapting existing properties. Yet the licensing framework has not kept pace with the scale and complexity of the modern renovation market.

The RRC proposal addresses this by:

  • Recognising real on-site experience — licensing that reflects demonstrated renovation capability rather than requiring carpenters to obtain a full builder’s licence for renovation-focused work
  • Supporting housing opportunity — reducing bottlenecks in the renovation supply chain and allowing experienced tradespeople to operate in a clearly defined, accountable framework
  • Improving regulatory fit — aligning the licensing structure with how renovation work is actually performed and contracted in the market
  • Protecting consumers — ensuring renovation work is undertaken by appropriately licensed practitioners with defined accountability and compliance obligations
  • Letting builders focus on what they do best — by separating renovation-focused work from new-home and major structural construction, builders can concentrate their capacity on larger projects
