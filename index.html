Industry-led reform proposal • Victoria, Australia

Victoria’s renovation system needs a smarter licence pathway.

Carpenters for Housing Reform Victoria is advocating for a new regulated licence class: Registered Residential Carpenter (RRC) — a practical middle-tier pathway between carpenter and builder for qualified, experienced renovation professionals.

The Problem

The current system no longer matches the economic reality of modern renovations.

Victoria’s residential building framework requires a registered builder for many projects over approximately $10,000. But in today’s market, ordinary renovation work regularly exceeds that amount.

$20k–$40k Bathrooms commonly sit far above the threshold
$25k–$70k Kitchens regularly fall into builder-only territory
$10k+ Decks, pergolas, and small renovations often exceed the current limit

Renovation bottlenecks

Small and medium renovation jobs are pushed into a builder-only system, creating friction and slowing delivery.

Experience not recognised

Skilled carpenters with years of on-site coordination experience cannot formally contract work they already lead.

Builders pulled downward

Registered builders are drawn into smaller renovation projects that could be handled under a properly regulated trade-led pathway.

The Solution

Introduce a Registered Residential Carpenter licence.

The proposed Registered Residential Carpenter (RRC) licence would create a regulated, accountable pathway for experienced carpenters to legally manage defined residential renovation work while preserving clear builder responsibilities.

This proposal is not about replacing builders. It is about recognising real experience, improving renovation capacity, and creating a smarter licensing structure for the modern residential market.

Suggested eligibility

A credible pathway should recognise both formal training and proven field experience.

  • Completed carpentry apprenticeship
  • Minimum 10+ years practical industry experience
  • Demonstrated renovation project coordination
  • Knowledge of compliance, permits, and sequencing
  • Understanding of trade coordination and site responsibility

What this reform achieves

It creates a practical middle-tier licence between carpenter and builder.

  • Recognises experienced renovation specialists
  • Supports builder capacity by separating project types
  • Improves access for homeowners
  • Maintains standards through regulation
  • Modernises a framework that no longer matches the market
Clear Division of Responsibility

A stronger system defines the right licence for the right work.

The RRC model works because it creates boundaries. Builders remain essential. Licensed plumbers and electricians remain unchanged. This proposal improves clarity, not confusion.

Builders remain responsible for

  • New home construction
  • Structural construction
  • Major extensions
  • Multi-storey buildings
  • Large residential developments

Registered Residential Carpenters could manage

  • Bathroom renovations
  • Kitchen renovations
  • Window and door upgrades
  • Decks and pergolas
  • Internal renovations
  • Small additions and non-structural works
  • Repair and rectification work
Why It Matters

This is productivity reform, workforce reform, and housing reform at the same time.

A smarter renovation licence pathway can strengthen the industry without lowering standards.

For Government

Increases workforce utilisation, improves regulatory fit, and helps unlock renovation capacity across the state.

For Industry

Recognises real on-site experience, reduces bottlenecks, and gives builders room to focus on larger and more complex work.

For Homeowners

Improves access to qualified renovation professionals, with clearer accountability and potentially more competitive pricing.

Safeguards & Common Questions

A practical reform must protect standards, builders, and consumers.

The Registered Residential Carpenter proposal is designed to improve regulatory fit while preserving clear builder responsibilities and consumer protections.

Will this reduce building standards?

No. The proposal strengthens accountability by recognising experienced renovation specialists within a defined and regulated framework rather than leaving renovation work in a grey area.

Does this replace registered builders?

No. Builders would remain responsible for new homes, structural work, major extensions, and larger residential projects. The proposal is intended to support, not replace, builder licensing.

Does this add unnecessary complexity?

No. It simplifies a mismatch that already exists by creating a clear middle-tier pathway between carpenter and builder for appropriate renovation work.

How would the scope stay clear?

The licence would apply to defined non-structural residential renovation work, while structural construction, major extensions, and new dwellings remain within builder scope.

What about homeowner protection?

Any serious reform should include appropriate insurance, written contracts, compliance requirements, and clear accountability for the work being performed.

Why is this needed now?

Because the economic reality of modern renovations has changed. Bathrooms, kitchens, decks, and internal works now regularly exceed thresholds that no longer reflect normal residential renovation practice.
