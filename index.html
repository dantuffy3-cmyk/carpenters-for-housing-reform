Carpenters for Housing Reform Victoria

A practical reform proposal for renovation carpentry in Victoria.

This initiative proposes a Registered Residential Carpenter pathway for defined renovation work in existing homes, with the aim of improving regulatory fit, recognising established on-site experience, and supporting renovation capacity while fully protecting domestic builder scope.

This proposal does not replace or dilute domestic builder scope. It proposes a bounded pathway for defined renovation carpentry work in existing homes, with explicit exclusions for new home construction, major structural work, broader project responsibility, and other builder-led functions.

The problem

The current licensing framework does not always fit the practical reality of renovation work.

A significant share of residential renovation activity in Victoria occurs in existing homes and involves practical, carpentry-led work that differs from new home construction and from larger builder-led projects. In many cases, the current framework does not provide a clean, proportionate recognition pathway for experienced practitioners whose work sits within that renovation setting.

The result is a regulatory mismatch. Experienced carpenters may be undertaking defined renovation work in practice, while the licensing structure does not clearly reflect the character, scale, and limits of that work.

The proposed solution

A Registered Residential Carpenter pathway for defined renovation work in existing homes.

The proposed Registered Residential Carpenter pathway is intended to improve regulatory fit by recognising a defined class of renovation carpentry work within clear boundaries. The aim is not to lower standards or create a parallel builder class. The aim is to better align recognition, accountability, and scope with the actual work being performed.

The proposal is focused on existing-home renovation, explicit exclusions, fit-for-purpose competency recognition, and a clearer distinction between bounded renovation carpentry work and the broader responsibilities that remain with domestic builders.

Policy position

The core reform position is simple.

Recognise real renovation practice

A licensing framework should better reflect the practical work carried out by highly experienced carpenters in existing-home renovation settings.

Keep the scope bounded

Any RRC pathway should be limited, clearly defined, and supported by explicit exclusions, competency requirements, and compliance settings.

Protect builder responsibility

Domestic builder scope should remain intact for new homes, major structural work, broader project responsibility, and other builder-led functions.

Builder scope protection

This reform is designed to support builders, not displace them.

The proposal does not seek to transfer core builder responsibilities into carpenter scope. It is intended to address a specific regulatory gap around defined renovation work in existing homes while preserving the legal, compliance, and project responsibilities that remain properly within builder scope.

  • No proposal to move new home construction into carpenter scope.
  • No proposal to dilute domestic builder responsibility.
  • No proposal to create open-ended structural authority.
  • No proposal to bypass compliance settings that apply to builder-led work.

Why this matters

Better regulatory fit can support renovation capacity and housing opportunity.

Renovation work in existing homes forms a substantial part of Victoria’s housing activity. A clearer and more appropriate pathway for defined renovation carpentry work could improve industry clarity, support workforce recognition, and help the system better match the way smaller residential renovation projects are actually delivered.

Done properly, this can support consumers, practitioners, and the broader housing system without weakening standards or reducing the role of registered builders.

Next step

Read the proposal, provide feedback, or support the consultation.

Feedback is being sought from carpenters, builders, trainers, suppliers, housing stakeholders, and others with practical experience in renovation and licensing settings.
