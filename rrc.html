Registered Residential Carpenter (RRC)

A practical, renovation-focused licence pathway for highly experienced Victorian carpenters managing defined residential renovation work, with a future pathway toward limited structural renovation endorsement in existing homes.

The RRC proposal is designed to recognise real renovation capability, protect builder scope, and improve consumer protection in the part of the market where experienced carpenters already solve complex problems in existing houses every day.

Overview

The Registered Residential Carpenter (RRC) is a proposed middle-tier licence for highly experienced carpenters who already manage defined residential renovation work in existing homes. It is not a builder licence and does not replace the role of registered builders. Instead, it creates a clear, regulated pathway that reflects real renovation capability and responsibility on site.

An RRC would be a qualified carpenter with substantial post-apprenticeship experience, proven renovation project delivery, and demonstrated understanding of permits, compliance, and trade coordination. The pathway is designed to recognise renovation specialists while keeping builder scope protected for new homes, major structural construction, large extensions, multi-storey work, and developments.

The Problem

Victoria’s current framework generally pushes domestic building work over the monetary threshold into the builder pathway, while structural work requires a registered practitioner regardless of cost. In practice, many experienced carpenters already coordinate renovation delivery, solve existing-house problems, and manage practical structural interfaces in older homes.

The system does not clearly recognise this specialist renovation role. The result is a mismatch:

  • Common renovation projects now routinely exceed legacy cost thresholds.
  • Experienced carpenters are often the ones solving on-site renovation issues, but without a licence category that reflects that reality.
  • Builders are pulled into smaller renovation work that could be safely managed by a properly licensed renovation-focused carpenter.

The choice is not between “builder or nothing”. The choice is between an outdated binary and a modern, renovation-focused pathway that reflects how work is actually delivered.

The Solution

The RRC proposal introduces a clearly defined, renovation-focused licence for highly experienced carpenters, with a future pathway toward a limited structural renovation endorsement in existing homes. The core principles are:

  • Recognise experienced renovation carpenters as a distinct professional category in Victoria.
  • Define a scope that covers non-structural and specified renovation work in existing homes, not new-home construction.
  • Protect builder scope for new homes, major structural work, large extensions, and multi-storey projects.
  • Allow, in future, a tightly scoped structural renovation endorsement for existing homes only, linked to permits and clear limits.

This is practical regulatory modernisation: the right licence for the right work, with clear boundaries, strong consumer protection, and a better match between regulation and the real renovation market.

What the RRC Could and Could Not Cover

Could cover
  • Bathrooms
  • Kitchens
  • Decks, pergolas and verandahs
  • Internal renovations
  • Repair and rectification work
  • Defined renovation work in existing homes
Would not cover
  • New homes
  • Major structural construction
  • Large extensions
  • Multi-storey residential builds
  • Large domestic building projects

Indicative Eligibility

The RRC pathway is aimed at highly experienced carpenters with a strong track record in residential renovation work. Indicative eligibility settings could include:

  • Completed carpentry apprenticeship and recognised trade qualification.
  • Minimum 10+ years post-apprenticeship experience in residential carpentry.
  • Demonstrated renovation project experience in existing homes, not just new builds.
  • Evidence of coordinating licensed trades where required (plumbing, electrical, etc.).
  • Demonstrated understanding of permits, compliance, sequencing, and documentation.

Consumer Protection

Consumer protection is central to the RRC proposal. The intent is to improve, not dilute, standards by creating a licence that fits renovation work more accurately. Key consumer protection features could include:

  • Clear licensing and registration requirements for RRCs, visible to consumers.
  • Defined scope of work, with explicit boundaries where a registered builder is required.
  • Permit-linked work where applicable, with clear responsibility for compliance.
  • Insurance and contractual requirements aligned with the RRC scope.
  • Regulatory oversight consistent with other licensed building practitioners.

By recognising experienced renovation carpenters in a formal pathway, consumers gain clearer accountability, better transparency, and a stronger match between licence type and project type.

Future pathway toward structural renovation endorsement

A key part of the long-term vision is a future, tightly defined structural renovation endorsement for RRCs in existing homes. This would not be a general structural licence. Instead, it would:

  • Apply only to existing residential homes, not new-home construction.
  • Be limited to clearly defined small-scale structural renovation tasks.
  • Be linked to permits, engineering where required, and strict compliance obligations.
  • Operate within explicit regulatory limits that preserve builder scope for major structural work.

The aim is to recognise the real structural problem-solving already occurring in renovation work, while keeping major structural and new-home responsibilities firmly with registered builders.
