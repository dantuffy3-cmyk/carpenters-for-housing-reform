Technical Proposal

Registered Residential Carpenter (RRC)

The Registered Residential Carpenter (RRC) is a proposed middle-tier licence under the Domestic Building Contracts Act 1995 (Vic) and associated regulations. It creates a defined registration category for experienced carpenters whose practice is limited to non-structural and light-structural renovation work in existing residential dwellings. The RRC does not modify, reduce, or replace the scope of work reserved for registered domestic builders.

⚠ Critical policy position:

The RRC proposal does not replace, dilute, or encroach upon domestic builder scope. All work that currently requires a registered builder continues to require a registered builder. The RRC creates a separate, renovation-specific category for work that sits below the builder threshold.

Policy Rationale

Why the RRC Pathway Is Proposed

Victorian licensing law does not currently provide a registration category that accurately reflects the work performed by experienced residential renovation carpenters. Under the existing framework, a carpenter who manages small-to-medium renovation projects in existing homes — bathrooms, kitchens, decks, pergolas — operates in a regulatory gap: the work is below the complexity threshold that justifies full domestic builder registration, yet there is no formal licence that recognises the contractor responsibility, site management, and consumer-facing obligations that such practitioners carry.

This gap produces three regulatory problems:

  1. Consumer exposure: Consumers engaging renovation carpenters have no licence category through which to verify competency or access statutory protections.
  2. Workforce misalignment: Highly experienced tradespeople are either unlicensed for the contracting role they perform, or hold a full builder registration that is disproportionate to their scope of practice.
  3. Capacity constraint: The absence of a recognised renovation pathway limits the regulated workforce available to deliver the residential renovation work required by Victoria's housing pipeline.

The RRC addresses each of these problems through a clearly defined licence category with explicit scope boundaries, eligibility criteria, and consumer protection obligations.

Scope Boundaries

Proposed Scope of the RRC Licence

The following table defines the proposed scope boundaries for the RRC licence. Column definitions are exhaustive for the purposes of this proposal; any ambiguous work type is to be treated as builder-led pending regulatory clarification.

Bathroom renovations in existing dwellings Construction of new dwellings Any works requiring a building permit issued to a builder
Kitchen renovations in existing dwellings Structural extensions that alter the building envelope New home construction (Class 1a buildings)
Deck construction (non-structural to existing building) Multi-storey or Class 2–9 building works Major structural alterations and additions
Pergola and veranda construction Works requiring structural engineering sign-off beyond scope of RRC-S endorsement Large residential developments

Note: Final scope boundaries are subject to regulatory drafting and consultation with the Victorian Building Authority (VBA) and relevant industry bodies.

Builder Scope Protection

Preservation of Domestic Builder Scope

The RRC licence is explicitly designed to operate below and separate from the scope of registered domestic builders. The following protections are integral to the proposal and are not negotiable:

  • No overlap with builder work: The RRC scope does not include any category of work that currently requires a registered domestic builder. Where work types are adjacent, the RRC boundary defaults to exclusion.
  • No builder substitution: An RRC holder cannot be engaged in place of a registered builder on a project that requires a builder. The two licences are not interchangeable.
  • Structural work excluded by default: Structural works, extensions, and any works that modify the primary structure of a dwelling remain outside RRC scope unless specifically covered by an approved RRC-S structural endorsement (see Section 8).
  • Contract value limits: A maximum contract value threshold will be prescribed by regulation to ensure RRC holders do not take on projects of a complexity or scale that warrants full builder registration.
  • Statutory hierarchy maintained: The existing hierarchy under the Building Act 1993 (Vic) and the Domestic Building Contracts Act 1995 (Vic) is unchanged. Builder obligations, statutory warranties, and the owner-builder framework are unaffected.
Eligibility Criteria

Pathway to RRC Registration

Eligibility for RRC registration is intended to recognise demonstrated competency and industry experience. Proposed criteria are as follows:

  1. Hold a Certificate III in Carpentry (CPC30220 or equivalent) or a recognised equivalent trade qualification accepted by the VBA.
  2. Demonstrate a minimum of five years of post-trade full-time experience in residential renovation carpentry, supported by documentary evidence (employment records, project references, or equivalent).
  3. Hold, or undertake to obtain within a prescribed period, required insurance coverage: public liability insurance and, where relevant, domestic building insurance as required by the Domestic Building Contracts Act 1995.
  4. Pass a knowledge assessment covering Victorian building law, licence scope boundaries, consumer rights and obligations, and relevant Building Code of Australia (BCA) requirements applicable to renovation work.
  5. Satisfy the VBA's fit and proper person requirements consistent with those applied to domestic builder applicants.
  6. Pay the prescribed application and registration fee as determined by the VBA under its published fee schedule.

The VBA will be the registering authority. Existing processes for registration, renewal, complaints, and disciplinary action will apply to RRC holders as they do to other licence categories.

Compliance & Consumer Protection

Regulatory Obligations and Consumer Safeguards

The RRC licence is structured to ensure that consumers engaging an RRC holder have equivalent or enhanced protections to those currently available in the renovation market.

  • Mandatory insurance: RRC holders will be required to maintain minimum public liability insurance and, where applicable, domestic building insurance consistent with the requirements of the Domestic Building Contracts Act 1995.
  • Written contracts: Where a contract meets the prescribed threshold, RRC holders will be required to provide written contracts that comply with applicable consumer protection legislation.
  • Statutory warranties: Domestic building statutory warranties will apply to work carried out by RRC holders within their licensed scope, as would apply if the work were carried out by a registered builder.
  • VBA complaints jurisdiction: The VBA will have jurisdiction to investigate complaints against RRC holders and to take disciplinary action, including suspension or cancellation of registration.
  • Scope enforcement: Performing or contracting work outside the RRC scope will constitute an offence under the building registration framework, consistent with existing penalties for unlicensed work.
  • Consumer disclosure: RRC holders will be required to disclose their licence category, registration number, and scope limitations to clients prior to entering a contract.
Future Framework

Structural Endorsement: Proposed RRC-S Category

This proposal notes the potential for a future optional structural endorsement (RRC-S) to be developed as a subsequent regulatory instrument, subject to separate consultation. The RRC-S is not part of the current proposal and is referenced here for transparency regarding the longer-term framework.

If adopted, the RRC-S endorsement would permit holders to undertake defined categories of limited structural renovation work in existing dwellings, subject to:

  • Additional competency requirements specific to structural carpentry in renovation contexts;
  • Mandatory engagement of a registered engineer or building surveyor for structural elements;
  • Permit-linked work boundaries enforced at the building permit stage;
  • Separate insurance requirements appropriate to structural risk; and
  • Exclusion of any structural work that forms part of a project that, in totality, requires a domestic builder.

The RRC-S, if implemented, would not change the scope of domestic builder registration. All work categories that require a builder under the current framework would continue to require a builder regardless of whether an RRC-S endorsement exists.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common Questions on the RRC Proposal

Does the RRC replace or reduce the scope of registered domestic builders?
No. The RRC does not replace, reduce, or overlap with domestic builder scope. All work that currently requires a registered builder continues to require a registered builder. The RRC creates a separate category for renovation work that sits below the builder threshold and that currently has no formal licence category.
Can an RRC holder take on work that requires a builder?
No. An RRC holder cannot be engaged as a contractor for work that requires a registered domestic builder. The two licence categories are not interchangeable. Performing work outside the RRC scope without a builder registration constitutes unlicensed work.
What gap does the RRC address?
Victorian licensing does not currently provide a formal registration category for experienced carpenters who manage renovation projects in existing homes below the builder threshold. The RRC provides a defined, regulated pathway for this existing segment of the workforce, improving consumer protection and regulatory clarity.
Who administers and enforces the RRC licence?
The Victorian Building Authority (VBA) would be the registering and enforcement authority for the RRC, consistent with its existing role for all building practitioner registrations in Victoria.
What consumer protections apply to RRC-engaged work?
The proposal includes mandatory insurance requirements, written contract obligations where applicable, domestic building statutory warranties, VBA complaints jurisdiction, and scope disclosure obligations. Consumer protections are not diminished relative to equivalent renovation work performed by a registered builder.
Is the RRC-S structural endorsement part of this proposal?
No. The RRC-S is a potential future regulatory instrument referenced for transparency. It is not part of the current consultation and would require separate regulatory development and consultation before it could be implemented.
Who is eligible to apply for the RRC?
Carpenters holding a Certificate III in Carpentry (or recognised equivalent), with a minimum of five years post-trade experience in residential renovation carpentry, who meet the VBA's fit and proper person, insurance, and knowledge assessment requirements.
