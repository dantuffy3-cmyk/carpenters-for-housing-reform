Registered Residential Carpenter (RRC)

A proposed renovation-focused licensing tier for experienced Victorian carpenters working in existing residential buildings. This document sets out the proposed scope, entry requirements, exclusions, and builder scope protections.

Proposal Overview

What the RRC licence would be

The Registered Residential Carpenter (RRC) is a proposed middle-tier licence within Victoria's residential construction licensing framework. It would sit between an apprentice-qualified carpenter (who works as a subcontractor) and a registered builder (who holds authority across all classes of residential construction).

The RRC licence would authorise a holder to directly contract, price, and manage non-structural and light-structural renovation work in existing residential buildings, within a clearly defined and bounded scope. It would not extend to new residential construction or to structural works that currently require a registered builder licence.

The licence is proposed to be administered by the Victorian Building Authority (VBA) under an amended regulatory framework, with entry requirements, ongoing obligations, and disciplinary provisions consistent with existing VBA licence categories.

Rationale

Why this tier is proposed

Residential renovation in Victoria is a distinct segment of the construction industry, characterised by work in occupied or recently occupied existing homes, with a risk profile, contractual structure, and skills base that differs from new home construction.

The current licensing framework does not include a pathway specifically calibrated to this segment. As a result, experienced renovation carpenters who manage and deliver non-structural renovation work do not have a licence category that accurately reflects the scope and responsibility of their practice.

A dedicated renovation licensing tier would:

  • Improve consumer protection by ensuring renovation work is performed by appropriately licensed and accountable practitioners
  • Increase the number of licensed practitioners available for residential renovation work, supporting housing improvement outcomes across Victoria
  • Provide regulatory clarity for homeowners engaging renovation carpenters directly
  • Recognise the distinct competencies required for renovation work in existing buildings
  • Strengthen industry accountability without expanding liability beyond the renovation scope
Entry Requirements

Proposed entry pathway

Entry to the RRC would be experience-based and competency-assessed. The following requirements are proposed for consultation:

  • Trade qualification: Certificate III in Carpentry (or equivalent recognised qualification)
  • Experience: Minimum five years of documented on-site renovation carpentry experience following trade qualification, with demonstrated project management responsibility
  • Competency assessment: Demonstrated competency in renovation project delivery, including scope of works, contract management, and consumer interface
  • Insurance: Public liability insurance appropriate to the licensed scope
  • Fit and proper person: Standard VBA fit and proper person criteria

Note: Specific thresholds (years of experience, assessment format, insurance minimums) are subject to regulatory determination and would be established through consultation with the VBA, industry associations, and relevant government agencies.

Builder Scope Protection

Registered builder scope is explicitly preserved

This proposal is designed to introduce a complementary renovation-specific tier. It does not seek to alter, qualify, or reduce the scope of the registered building licence in any way.

The following categories of work would remain exclusively within registered builder scope under this proposal, without exception:

  • Construction of new residential dwellings
  • Major structural alterations, including load-bearing wall removal and structural footing works
  • Extensions and additions that constitute new habitable space
  • Multi-storey residential buildings (Class 2 and above under the NCC)
  • Commercial, industrial, and mixed-use construction
  • Any project where the scope, value, or risk profile requires a registered building licence under Victorian legislation

Wherever a building permit is required for work that exceeds the RRC scope, a registered builder would be required. The RRC licence does not grant authority to circumvent permit obligations or to perform works that require a higher licence category.

Industry associations representing registered builders are invited to engage with this proposal. The proponent welcomes formal comment and is open to further scope restrictions where legitimate concerns are identified.

Future Consideration

Optional structural endorsement (RRC-S)

This proposal notes, as a secondary consideration only, the possible future development of an optional structural endorsement — the RRC-S — that could allow RRC licence holders to perform defined structural renovation works under strict conditions.

An RRC-S endorsement is not part of this primary proposal. It is raised to acknowledge that the renovation sector includes a class of light structural work (such as non-load-bearing internal wall removal in single-storey existing homes) that may warrant a defined endorsement pathway in future, subject to:

  • Separate competency assessment and demonstrated structural renovation experience
  • Engineer oversight requirements for structurally significant elements
  • Regulatory determination of appropriate scope limits and conditions
  • Consultation with registered builder industry associations
  • No overlap with or reduction of existing registered builder authority

The RRC-S concept is presented here for completeness only. Any future structural endorsement pathway would require separate regulatory consultation and would not form part of the initial RRC implementation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about the RRC proposal

Does the RRC replace the registered building licence?

No. The RRC is a separate, bounded tier for renovation work in existing homes. The registered building licence remains the appropriate instrument for new construction, structural works, and all work that currently requires a builder licence. The RRC does not affect or diminish that scope in any way.

Would an RRC holder be able to do any structural work?

No, under the proposed RRC licence. Structural works — including load-bearing wall removal, footing works, structural roof framing, and structural extensions — are explicitly excluded. A possible future RRC-S endorsement is noted as a secondary consideration, but is not part of this proposal.

Why not simply require renovation carpenters to obtain a builder licence?

The registered building licence is calibrated to a broader scope and greater risk profile than non-structural renovation work. Requiring carpenters who operate exclusively in the renovation segment to meet full builder licence requirements — including assessment categories, insurance obligations, and competency requirements designed for new construction — may not represent a proportionate regulatory response. A renovation-specific tier allows regulatory requirements to be better matched to the actual scope and risk of the work.

How would consumer protection be maintained?

RRC holders would be registered with the VBA, subject to the same fit and proper person requirements as other licence holders, required to carry appropriate public liability insurance, and subject to VBA disciplinary processes. Homeowners would be able to verify an RRC licence through the VBA register, as with any other licensed practitioner.

What oversight body would administer the RRC?

This proposal contemplates administration by the Victorian Building Authority (VBA) under an amended regulatory framework. The specific administrative arrangements, including licence register structure, fee schedule, and disciplinary procedures, would be subject to regulatory determination.

Who is putting forward this proposal?

This proposal is put forward by Daniel Sonsie, Director of Sonsie Carpentry Pty Ltd, a Victorian carpentry business specialising in residential renovation. It is an industry-initiated consultation draft, not a government proposal. It is intended to prompt formal policy consideration and welcomes engagement from all parts of the industry.
