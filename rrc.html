Registered Residential Carpenter (RRC)

A renovation-focused middle-tier licensing pathway for highly experienced carpenters working in existing-home residential renovation — improving regulatory fit, protecting builder scope, and expanding Victoria’s renovation capacity.

Supporting builders. Recognising real on-site experience. Expanding renovation capacity.

The RRC does not replace builders. It creates a clearly bounded, renovation-focused licence for experienced carpenters in the existing-home sector — improving outcomes for homeowners, trades, and the broader housing system.

What is the RRC?

A renovation-focused middle-tier licensing pathway

The Registered Residential Carpenter (RRC) is a proposed middle-tier licence designed specifically for carpenters who specialise in existing-home renovation. Unlike the current framework — which offers only a basic carpenter registration or a full builder’s licence — the RRC would sit between these two tiers, recognising the distinct skill set required for renovation-focused work.

Victoria’s housing system relies heavily on renovation activity. Bathrooms, kitchens, decks, pergolas, internal fit-outs, and window or door upgrades form a substantial part of the residential work being carried out every year. Yet highly experienced carpenters who specialise in this work have no licensing pathway that properly reflects their capabilities or the work they perform.

The RRC proposal addresses that gap — creating a licence that is genuinely fit for purpose, properly regulated, and clearly bounded to protect both consumers and existing builder scope.

Existing homes only RRC work is strictly limited to renovation of existing residential dwellings
Non-structural core The base RRC covers non-structural renovation work as its primary scope
Builder scope protected New homes, major structural work, and large developments remain builder territory
Why it exists

Why the RRC exists

Renovation work is a major and growing part of Victoria’s housing activity. Homeowners are increasingly choosing to renovate rather than move — yet the current licensing framework does not clearly recognise the role of renovation-specialist carpenters.

  • Many highly experienced renovation carpenters have no licence that fits what they actually do
  • The current system creates a gap between basic carpenter registration and a full builder’s licence
  • Renovation work requires specialist judgment, trade coordination, and existing-home problem-solving
  • Homeowners benefit when experienced trades are properly recognised and regulated
  • The broader housing system benefits from improved renovation productivity
Recognition

What the RRC would recognise

The RRC is specifically designed for carpenters with deep, proven renovation experience — not new entrants. It recognises the practical skills and judgment that come with years of existing-home renovation work.

  • Extensive renovation experience across multiple project types
  • Capability to independently manage non-structural renovation projects
  • Understanding of compliance, permits, and regulatory obligations
  • Skills in trade sequencing, coordination, and project problem-solving
  • Ability to work safely and effectively within existing home constraints
  • Consumer protection through clear accountability and regulated practice
Division of responsibility

Clear division of responsibility

A well-designed RRC framework depends on a clear, enforceable division between what an RRC can do and what requires a registered builder. This division is defined by the type of work, the nature of the building, and the scope of the project.

Builder scope

Builder scope remains protected

The RRC is not a builder’s licence and it is not designed to replace builders. Builder scope is fully protected under the RRC framework.

Builder scope — not affected by the RRC
  • New home construction (all types)
  • Major structural construction in any building
  • Major extensions to existing residential homes
  • Multi-storey residential or commercial buildings
  • Large-scale residential or commercial developments
  • Any work that falls outside clearly defined renovation categories

The RRC carves out a specific, bounded lane for renovation-focused carpenters — it does not encroach on the work that builders do best. Builders remain the appropriate licence holder for the high-complexity, structural, and new-build work that defines their profession.

Future direction

Future structural endorsement under strict limits

The core RRC licence is deliberately non-structural. However, we recognise that highly experienced renovation carpenters sometimes encounter minor structural elements as part of otherwise non-structural renovation projects in existing homes.

A future optional endorsement — available only to RRC holders who meet additional criteria — could allow limited structural renovation work in existing homes, subject to:

  • Strict scope limits defined by regulation
  • Permit-linked oversight and council inspection requirements
  • Engineer sign-off where required by regulation
  • Clear safeguards to protect consumers and maintain standards
  • No blurring of the line between renovation carpenters and registered builders

This endorsement would not be automatic, would not be available to all RRCs, and would not extend to new construction or the full scope of builder work. It represents a careful, measured recognition of a specific real-world need in the renovation sector.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

Does the RRC replace builders?

No. Builder scope remains fully protected. The RRC is a separate, bounded licence for renovation-focused carpenters in the existing-home sector only.

Who qualifies for an RRC?

The RRC is for highly experienced carpenters with a proven track record in existing-home residential renovation — not new entrants or general-purpose carpenters.

Can an RRC build new homes?

No. New home construction is explicitly outside RRC scope and remains the domain of registered builders only.

Why focus on existing-home renovation?

Renovation of existing homes is a distinct discipline requiring specialist judgment, problem-solving, and trade coordination that differs significantly from new construction.

Will RRCs be allowed to do structural work?

The core RRC is non-structural. A future optional endorsement may allow limited structural renovation work in existing homes, under strict permit-linked conditions and safeguards.

Can RRCs do major extensions?

No. Major extensions to existing homes remain within builder scope. The RRC is for defined renovation categories only.

Is this proposal supported by trades?

Support is growing among experienced Victorian carpenters and renovation trades who recognise the need for a better-matched licensing pathway.

How does the RRC protect consumers?

By bringing experienced renovation carpenters into a regulated licensing framework, the RRC creates clear accountability, defined scope, and improved consumer protection.

Who is behind this initiative?

The proposal is led by Daniel Sonsie, a Victorian carpenter advocating for practical, renovation-focused licensing reform that benefits trades, homeowners, and the housing system.
