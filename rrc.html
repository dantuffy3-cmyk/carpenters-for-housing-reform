Registered Residential Carpenter (RRC)

A renovation‑focused middle‑tier licensing pathway for highly experienced carpenters working in existing‑home residential renovation — improving regulatory fit, protecting builder scope, and supporting housing opportunity and renovation productivity across Victoria.

Overview

What is the Registered Residential Carpenter?

The Registered Residential Carpenter (RRC) is a proposed middle‑tier licence for carpenters who have dedicated their careers to existing‑home residential renovation. It recognises real‑world capability across bathrooms, kitchens, decks, pergolas, verandas, internal renovations, window and door upgrades, and other non‑structural residential renovation work.

The RRC is specifically scoped to renovation of existing homes. It is not a backdoor into builder territory. Builder scope remains protected — new homes, major structural construction, major extensions, multi‑storey buildings, and large developments stay firmly within the domain of registered builders.

Why the RRC

Why the RRC exists — and what it recognises

Why the RRC exists

Renovation work is a major and growing part of Victoria's housing system. Thousands of experienced carpenters specialise in this space — yet the current licensing framework was not designed around the realities of renovation‑focused work.

The result is a regulatory gap: experienced renovation carpenters lack a licence that accurately reflects what they do, and clients lack a clear credential to look for when hiring for renovation work.

  • Recognises highly experienced renovation carpenters
  • Improves regulatory fit for existing‑home renovation
  • Provides clarity for consumers and tradespeople alike
  • Supports housing opportunity and renovation productivity
  • Protects builder scope for major structural work
What the RRC recognises

The RRC is designed for carpenters who have accumulated deep, demonstrated experience in residential renovation — not entry‑level practitioners.

  • Deep, proven renovation experience
  • Capability to scope, price, and manage non‑structural renovation projects
  • Understanding of compliance, building permits, and sequencing
  • Trade coordination and client communication skills
  • Commitment to consumer protection and professional standards
  • Work that does not replace or undermine registered builders
Scope & boundaries

RRC scope vs builder scope — a clear division

The RRC is defined by a clear and legally maintained boundary. There is no ambiguity: renovation carpenters work within RRC scope, and builders retain their protected scope without overlap.

RRC scope — renovation of existing homes
  • Bathroom renovations
  • Kitchen renovations
  • Decks, pergolas, verandas
  • Internal renovations and fitouts
  • Window and door upgrades
  • Flooring, cladding, and internal carpentry
  • Non‑structural residential renovation work
  • Minor alterations within existing structural envelopes
Builder scope — remains fully protected
  • New homes (all new residential construction)
  • Major structural construction
  • Major extensions
  • Multi‑storey buildings
  • Large developments and subdivisions
  • New structural additions to existing homes
  • Any work currently requiring a builder's licence
🔒

Builder scope is protected by design. The RRC is not a partial builder's licence. It applies exclusively to renovation of existing homes and does not grant any rights to undertake work that currently requires a registered builder. This boundary is central to the proposal and non‑negotiable.

Future direction

Future structural endorsement — strict limits only

The core RRC licence is non‑structural. The proposal contemplates a possible future structural endorsement that could allow a subset of RRC holders to undertake limited structural renovation work in existing homes — but only under strictly defined conditions.

Strict limits

Any structural endorsement would be bounded by clear scope caps — limited to minor structural alterations in existing single‑storey residential dwellings, with explicit exclusions.

Clear safeguards

Structural work under endorsement would be subject to mandatory engineer sign‑off, building permit requirements, and independent inspection — no self‑certification of structural elements.

No blurring of lines

The endorsement is designed so that the boundary between RRC holders and registered builders remains clear, enforceable, and publicly understood. Major structural work stays with builders.

Housing & productivity

Why this supports housing opportunity and renovation productivity

Victoria faces significant housing pressure. Making better use of existing housing stock — through renovation, adaptation, and upgrade — is a key part of the solution. The RRC proposal directly supports this by improving the regulatory environment for renovation‑focused work.

↑ Renovation capacity supported by a dedicated licence pathway
🏠 Existing homes upgraded and adapted more efficiently
✓ Consumer protection through clear credentialling
🔒 Builder scope fully protected throughout
Better regulatory fit

The current framework was not designed around renovation‑focused carpenters. The RRC gives experienced tradespeople a licence that accurately reflects their work, reducing compliance friction and improving professional accountability.

Renovation productivity

A dedicated licence pathway reduces barriers for experienced renovation carpenters to operate professionally and competitively — improving throughput of renovation work across Victoria's housing market.

Housing opportunity

Consumer confidence

The RRC gives consumers a clear credential to look for when hiring for renovation work — improving transparency and trust in the renovation market without undermining builder‑led projects.

FAQ

Frequently asked questions

Does the RRC replace builders?

No. The RRC is a middle‑tier licence specifically for renovation of existing homes. Builder scope — new homes, major structural construction, major extensions, multi‑storey buildings, and large developments — remains fully protected and is explicitly excluded from RRC scope.

Who is eligible for the RRC?

The RRC is designed for highly experienced carpenters who have dedicated significant professional time to residential renovation work. Entry requirements would reflect genuine experience and demonstrated capability — not simply certificate completion.

Why focus specifically on existing‑home renovation?

Renovation of existing homes is a distinct skill set. It requires practical judgment, problem‑solving within existing structures, compliance understanding, sequencing, and trade coordination — different from new‑home construction. The current framework does not adequately reflect this specialisation.

Will RRC holders be allowed to do structural work?

The core RRC licence is non‑structural. A future optional structural endorsement is contemplated — but only under strict limits, mandatory engineer sign‑off, building permits, and independent inspection. It would not allow major structural work, which stays with builders.

Does the RRC apply to new home construction?

No. The RRC applies exclusively to renovation of existing residential homes. New home construction is builder scope and is not proposed to change.

How does the RRC support housing opportunity?

By improving the regulatory framework for renovation‑focused carpenters, the RRC supports increased renovation capacity — helping more Victorian homeowners access quality tradework to upgrade, adapt, and improve their existing homes. This contributes to broader housing livability and opportunity without impacting new‑home delivery.

Who is behind this proposal?

The proposal is led by Daniel Sonsie, founder of Carpenters for Housing Reform Victoria — a Victorian carpenter advocating for practical, renovation‑focused licensing reform that supports the trades, protects consumers, and improves housing outcomes.
