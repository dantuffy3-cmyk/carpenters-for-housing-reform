Registered Residential Carpenter Proposal

A bounded proposal for renovation carpentry in existing homes.

The Registered Residential Carpenter (RRC) is a proposed reform model intended to improve regulatory fit for experienced carpenters undertaking defined residential renovation work in existing homes, while fully protecting domestic builder scope and responsibility.

This proposal does not replace or dilute domestic builder scope. It proposes a defined and limited pathway for renovation carpentry work in existing homes, with explicit exclusions for new home construction, major structural work, broader project responsibility, and builder-led functions.

What the RRC is

A proposal to better align licensing with real renovation practice.

The RRC is a proposed reform model for highly experienced carpenters who principally work on residential renovation and alteration projects in existing homes. The intent is to recognise established on-site capability within a clearly defined scope, rather than force a mismatch between the work being performed and the regulatory pathways currently available.

The proposal is designed to support better regulatory fit, clearer accountability, stronger consumer confidence, and more efficient delivery of renovation work where a full domestic builder model may not reflect the actual nature of the work being undertaken.

Why it exists

The current system does not cleanly fit a large share of existing-home renovation work.

Victoria has a substantial pool of experienced carpenters delivering renovation and alteration work in existing homes. In practice, many of these jobs involve defined carpentry-led works, limited coordination, and modest project scale, yet the current licensing framework does not always provide a pathway that reflects this work clearly or proportionately.

The result is a regulatory mismatch: experienced practitioners can be left without an appropriate recognition pathway, while consumers and industry operate in an environment that would benefit from clearer boundaries, responsibilities, and capability recognition.

Proposed scope boundaries

Defined scope, explicit exclusions, and preserved builder responsibility.

Defined renovation carpentry in existing homes New home construction Full domestic building responsibility

Alteration work that remains within a clearly bounded residential renovation framework Large extensions, complex structural reconfiguration, and multi-dwelling work Coordination of works where builder-led compliance obligations apply

Final scope boundaries would need to be set through formal regulatory design, including clear monetary, technical, structural, and compliance limits.

Builder protection

Builder scope remains intact.

The proposal is not intended to create a parallel builder class. It is intended to define a more appropriate pathway for a limited category of renovation carpentry work in existing homes. Domestic builders would continue to hold exclusive responsibility for projects and work types that properly belong within builder scope.

  • No change to the core role or legal responsibility of registered builders.
  • No proposal to transfer new home construction into carpenter scope.
  • No proposal to remove builder control from major structural or high-risk residential work.
  • No proposal to bypass broader compliance obligations that sit with builder-led projects.

Eligibility pathway

Recognition should be based on demonstrated experience and defined competency.

A workable RRC pathway should recognise real on-site experience, not only classroom-based progression. A formal model could combine trade qualification, verified residential renovation experience, referee evidence, compliance history, and any targeted gap training needed for the defined RRC scope.

The goal is not to lower standards. The goal is to align standards with the actual work class being recognised.

Consumer protection and compliance

A better fit should still mean clear accountability.

Any RRC pathway should sit inside a clear consumer protection framework. That includes defined scope limits, registration conditions, complaint and disciplinary coverage, evidence of competency, and straightforward rules about when work must remain under builder-led delivery.

A properly bounded pathway can improve clarity for consumers by making it easier to understand what a practitioner is authorised to do, what sits outside that authority, and when a registered builder remains required.

Future endorsement

Any structural endorsement would need to be separate, limited, and tightly controlled.

A future structural endorsement, if considered at all, should be treated as a separate policy question rather than assumed within the base RRC model. It would require clearly defined limits, stronger competency requirements, and specific regulatory safeguards.

For the purposes of this proposal, the core RRC concept should remain focused on defined renovation work in existing homes and should not rely on an expansive structural scope.

FAQ

Common questions

Does the RRC replace domestic builders?

No. The proposal is expressly designed to preserve builder scope and responsibility.

Is this a builder licence by another name?

No. The proposal is for a defined and narrower work class focused on existing-home renovation carpentry, with explicit exclusions and preserved builder-led functions.

Why is this reform being proposed?

Because a significant portion of residential renovation work does not fit neatly within the current licensing structure, despite being carried out by experienced practitioners in real-world settings.

Who is the RRC intended for?

Experienced carpenters with substantial practical history in residential renovation work in existing homes.

Would consumers still be protected?

Yes. The purpose of the proposal is to improve regulatory clarity, defined accountability, and fit-for-purpose recognition within a proper compliance framework.
